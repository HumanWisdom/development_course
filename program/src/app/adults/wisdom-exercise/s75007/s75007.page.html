Exploring judgment

Welcome to this session where we are going to explore how our mind is always judging ourselves and others, the impact of that on our lives, and how we may respond with wisdom. Keep in mind the basic principles of enquiry:

  • We are just looking and learning about ourselves
  • Have a mind open to learning
  • For learning to take place we need to pause judgement
  • What we observe in ourselves is not right or wrong, good or bad - it just needs to be understood
  • Deep down, we are all the same human beings

Exploring judgement. Why do we judge ourselves and others?

Welcome to this session. We are going to explore how our mind judges all the time, why it does that, and the implications of this for our lives.

We judge ourselves, judge others, and the world around us as good or bad, right or wrong – our mind does that unconsciously and automatically. We are also worried about how others judge us – and that can create a feeling of insecurity, or a need to put on a mask so we can control what others think of us.

Every time we pass judgement and form an opinion it strengthens the sense of ‘me’ – notice the subtle pleasure you get from expressing your opinion.

We judge our thoughts and our feelings, our achievements and failures.

Judgement is important because it allows us to make sense of the world and to protect us from harm, but as we will explore, it can also cause problems and stop us from making the most of our short time on earth. A deeper understanding of what is going on in our thinking allows us to be free, and respond with wisdom

The process of judgement in our thinking is automatic and unconscious. It serves an important function in our lives, but can also cause problems for ourselves and others. The fear of being judged can make us feel anxious.

Be curious, not judgemental.

Walt Whitman

Judging the world

Our mind constantly judges the world around us as good or bad, right or wrong. Think of the news you read or listen to – you will have an opinion on most things that you come across – even if they are completely outside your zone of control and not impacting you in any way.

The opinions we form then affect how we feel and we can get distressed reading the news – and this can stop us noticing the beauty of the world we live in.

We are usually certain our opinions are correct and others with a different opinion must be wrong.

By noticing how our mind judges the world constantly we can ask if we really need to do that, how it affects us, and how it serves us.

Just as we look at a tree, or our breathing, it is possible to look at the world without automatically judging everything as good or bad, right or wrong – this allows us to see things as they are, and stops the chattering of the mind, allowing us to notice the beauty around us.

We automatically judge people who have different opinions to our own and feel closer to people who think like us.

What is going on in our thinking when we pass judgement?

What is going on in our thinking when we pass judgement – and why do we do that all the time?

We all carry our memories from childhood and we use this to make sense of the world and interact with it. Our mind compares what it sees with what it knows and there is an automatic reaction of judgement if something is different.

This process is very useful to help us make sense of the world, do our daily tasks and avoid danger.

The same process continues when it no longer serves our needs – passing judgement on ourselves, others, and the world.

Understanding what is going on in the background gives us choices, and we can decide when we need to have an opinion and when we don’t.

Ask yourself - Do I really need to have an opinion on this person or situation? - can I just accept things as they are?

Judging ourselves

Just as our mind judges the world around us, we also judge ourselves - as good or bad, or right or wrong. This can make us arrogant - thinking we are always right, or the best, or it can make us self-critical - thinking we are not good enough, are a failure, or ruminate over what we have done in the past.

Neither of these serves us, because this judgement blocks our learning about ourselves - which is the doorway to wisdom.

It seems challenging for us to look at ourselves as we are - without judging ourselves. If I observe I am not a good listener or I am overweight I can explore why, and do something about it - but if I feel bad and start criticising myself - all my energy is taken up being critical - and the problem remains

Judging ourselves can make us arrogant or self-critical - both don't serve us and can stop us learning about ourselves and growing in wisdom.

Excessively self-criticism can make us:

  • Have low self-esteem
  • Unhappy
  • Anxious to try new things
  • Not feel worthy of love
  • Lack confidence
  • Create a loop of thinking negatively about yourself

The worst loneliness is not to be comfortable with yourself

Mark Twain

Judging others.

Just as we judge ourselves and the world around us, we also judge the people we meet. We do this unconsciously - even before they have said a word, for example the clothes people wear or their skin color. Our judgements are more a reflection of our own conditioning than who others really are. We can take a dislike to people without realising why, or the other way around.

We give people a label, categorise them into different groups and then assume they are the same as everyone else in that group - and this is how prejudices get established.

We are usually pretty certain about our judgement of others and rarely question our view. Others are equally quick to judge us, but we rarely know what they actually think.

We prefer to be with people who are like us, and try and avoid people whom we regard as different.

Everytime we judge others, we strengthen the ‘me’ which brings a certain pleasure.

Everything that irritates us about others can lead us to a deeper understanding of ourselves.

Carl Jung.

Feeling judged

Just as we judge others, we also worry how others judge us. We assume we know what they are thinking.

Sometimes this can create anxiety. We want to be liked, and cannot bear the thought of anyone not liking us. This is because we are afraid that if we are not liked we will end up alone and that feels frightening.

This anxiety can impact our lives in many ways - we may try to avoid meeting people for fear of being judged by them, or be afraid to share our ideas with others. We may put on a mask and try to be a person that we think others will like - but behind the mask our anxiety continues . We may even leave jobs where we are worried about being judged - like teaching where there is constant assessment.

We assume others' judgments of us are correct - both positive and negative - and this reinforces what we believe about ourselves - but it may not be true.

We can be certain that we know what others are thinking - even when people tell us we are mistaken.

We think others are thinking of us and we also assume to know what they are thinking. This can create a feeling of anxiety.

We assume others' judgments of us are correct - both positive and negative - and this reinforces what we believe about ourselves.

Feeling judged by others can affect us in many ways:

  • Feel anxious what others are thinking
  • Pretend to be someone we think will be more likeable
  • Assume we know what others are thinking
  • Avoid doing things where we may be judged

Overcoming the anxiety of being judged by others.

How can we overcome the anxiety of being judged by others?

The first is to realise that people’s judgement of us is a reflection of their conditioning, and not who we really are. They are also judging automatically and are not aware of the process in their own thinking.

The second is to realise that people rarely have any time to think about us - because they are too busy thinking about themselves - that's just the way our mind is wired.

And the third is to realise that no matter what you do people will think something - and it is not something we can control. We can see how the fear of being judged is shaping our life and decide that it does not serve us - and this can free us to walk our own path in life.

We could realise that our mind has a negative bias and gives much more weight to negative comments than to positive ones.

The people you are close to and love you will love you anyway, and you will attract the people who like you for who you really are - so relax, and just be yourself. There is such a huge relief in doing that.

How can we be free of the anxiety of feeling judged by others?

  • Realise its a reflection of their conditioning, and not who you are
  • Realise people are usually preoccupied with themselves
  • You cannot control what people think - so why try?
  • Being authentic will allow the right people to find you
