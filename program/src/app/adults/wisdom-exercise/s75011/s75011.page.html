Zooming Out & In

Welcome to this session where we are going to learn a useful practical skill - how to zoom out of difficult situations and regain perspective, and how to zoom in when a situation demands our complete attention. This can help us deal with stress, and boost our communication and relationships skills. Keep in mind the basic principles of enquiry:

  • Be curious - we are just looking and learning about ourselves
  • For learning to take place we need to pause judgement
  • For learning to take place we need to pause judgement
  • What we observe in ourselves is not right or wrong, good or bad
  • Deep down, we are all the same human beings

Learn a new skill - Zooming In and Zooming Out

In this session we are going to explore a new skill - that of learning when to zoom out of situations and the importance of that, and when to zoom in, and pay closer attention.

It’s basically consciously doing the opposite of what the mind normally does, and knowing when is the right time to do that.

For example, when we are listening to someone it helps to zoom in and give the person our complete attention. When we are stressed because our train is late, zooming out is useful - so we can see the bigger picture and realise that these things happen from time to time.

In a series of exercises we are going to explore where this skill can be applied and how it can benefit you

Benefits of learning to Zoom Out & Zoom In

  • Deal with stress and anxiety
  • Make better decisions
  • Avoid conflict in your relationships
  • Buy what you need
  • Deal with fear
  • Pay attention to the life you live

Sam sees a jacket he really likes

Sam is 26 and goes shopping to buy some shoes. After he buys his shoes he is wandering around the shopping centre and sees a jacket in the shop window that he really likes the look of.

He debates whether to go in, because he already has enough jackets at home, but goes in and convinces himself that he doesn’t have a jacket quite like this one - and buys it. He is really pleased with himself and wears it to go home. Once he gets home his wife tells him off for buying yet another jacket! He sees all his jackets and realises he hasn’t worn some of the jackets for months.

How could zooming out have helped Sam?

If Sam had been able to zoom out of the situation, he could have seen the bigger picture

  • That perhaps he has more jackets than he needs, and could save the money - he is in debt anyway. When we are faced with a decision where pleasure is involved, it can bypass our critical thinking and push us to get whatever it is we think will make us happy - but it is not going to last for long
  • He is not the first person to go to the shops to buy one thing, and come back with more things than he can afford, or needs.
  • Zooming out allows us to pause and ask ourselves if we really need to buy that, if we can afford it, what impact that is having on the planet, and so on. Will you still feel the same the next day?

Benefits of zooming out when we go shopping:

  • We avoid buying things we do not need
  • We can save money
  • We know the pleasure will be short-lived
  • We do our bit for the planet
  • We don’t up the house

The best things in life aren’t things.

Art Buchwald

David’s girlfriend says she loves someone

David is 21 and in university. He is popular and good at his studies. He has been seeing his girl-friend for over a year now and is in love with her. She is one of the prettiest girls in his class and all his friends are envious and tell him that.

Over the last few weeks they have been having some rows, because he has been busy with his football and not had enough time to see her.

He is astonished when one day she announces that she has fallen in love with another boy in their class - someone in his circle of friends.

He is completely heartbroken, and very stressed. He has a complex mix of emotions - sadness from losing his girlfriend who he loves, embarrassment and loss of face with his friends, and feeling a loss of confidence because he does not feel worthy of being loved. He goes into a depression, not talking to anyone.

How could zooming out help David?

When we are stressed all our focus is on the event - that becomes our world. If we fail an exam we think our whole life is a failure.

David feels no one will ever love him again and he will never recover. He feels he cannot show his face to his friends.

If David could zoom out of the situation he would realise that people will come and go from our lives, and this is all part of the cycle of life. He could ask himself what there is to learn from the relationship so he can grow as a person. He is still loved by many friends and by his family and he knows he will find love again. In any case love is not a prison, and there is no point being with someone who does not want to be with you.

When we are stressed, zooming out can help us see the bigger picture, and realise we still have so much to be grateful for.

Benefits of zooming out when you are stressed:

  • Have time to pause before you act
  • Avoid making bad decisions
  • See the big picture
  • Realise that this too will pass
  • Consider what you have to be grateful for

It all depends on how we look at things, and not how they are in themselves.

Carl Jung

Sophie is afraid of her final exams

Sophie is 18 and in her final year of high school.

She has exams coming up and is really worried about not doing well in them. She needs to get good grades to get into university to study Medicine. There is a lot of pressure from her parents to study and do well and she feels there is too much to learn.

Besides, memorising things has never been her strength.

She is so worried about her exams, she starts feeling anxious, and gets palpitations. She is worried what her parents will think if she does not do well - and what she will do if she does not get into Medicine. She starts taking tablets for her anxiety - but they don’t really help. She finds it difficult to sleep at night

Have you ever been anxious about exams, or something else? Would zooming out have helped you in that situation?

How would zooming out help Sophie?

How could consciously zooming out of the situation help Sophie?

If you have been to school or college - you would have experienced the challenge of exams.

When we are afraid, as Sophie is in this case of failure, zooming out helps put things in perspective. She could see that most worries are imaginary and rarely come to pass. Worrying is not going to solve anything anyway, and is interfering with her preparation for her exams. Even if she did not get the grades to do medicine - there are other options - she could try again the next year, or do something else - and have an equally great career doing that.

The fear of failure can cause anxiety, which reduces our ability to think clearly, and our performance - zooming out and seeing the big picture offers us fresh ways of dealing with it.

Benefits of zooming out when you are anxious:

  • Put the event into perspective
  • Realise it does not matter as much as you think it does
  • Realise that even if it did happen, you would be okay
  • Realise that most worries never come to pass
  • You can deal more effectively with the situation

A thought is not a fact. Don’t believe everything you think.

Simon and Paula are having a fight.

Simon and Paula are in their 30’s and have been married for 5 years. They are both working - Simon works as a teacher, and Paula works part-time in a local shop. They have 2 small children.

They have been having quite a few arguments lately - about different things. Paula feels Simon does not do enough around the house, and Simon feels unloved and constantly criticised by Paula. They are both stressed for different reasons - Simon because of the pressures of work, and Paula because she has to juggle a job and look after the kids.

Today, they are having a familiar argument. Simon is watching TV and Paula asks him to clean the kitchen, and Simon says he is tired and will do it later. She erupts with anger. Simon feels he has had enough, and begins to think of leaving the relationship

How could zooming out help Simon and Paula?

All of us are in a relationship - with our parents, friends, and family members - disagreements and arguments are normal.

When an argument occurs our mind focuses on the emotional distress we are feeling and goes into reactive mode - we lose control over what we are saying - and situations can turn ugly.

Zooming out would have helped Simon see that overall they have a good relationship and think about the wonderful person Paula is most of the time. He would have the space to reflect on what emotional needs Paula has that are not being met - is she upset about something more than a clean kitchen? They have wonderful children, and perhaps he could make more of an effort in the relationship.

Zooming out may stop their relationship breaking up

Benefits of zooming out in an argument

  • Avoid saying things you may regret
  • See the bigger picture
  • Realise that there is much to appreciate about the other person
  • Reflect on what the other person is going through
  • Be able to come back to the topic when you have calmed down

Nicola needs to decide whether to own up to making a mistake

Nicola is 25 and works in a company

Last week her boss asked her to send some payments out to their suppliers.

She was just looking over her records and realises she has made a mistake. Instead of sending £100 to a supplier, she has made a cheque for £1000 - she must have been busy and not focused.

Now she is in a dilemma and very stressed. She has made a mistake and is not sure what to do next. If she tells her boss she is sure he will be very angry, and she may even lose her job. She is wondering if she can just keep quiet about it, and perhaps no one will notice. It's a big company, and no one may pick that up.

Benefits of zooming out for Nicola

When we are faced with a challenge, we can feel under pressure, and worry about the consequences for ourselves. Telling a small lie is very tempting, especially as this can help us get out of the situation right now.

In this case if Nicola is able to zoom out she would realise that it takes courage to own up to our mistakes - but it is the right thing to do - and in the long-term the mistake does not matter - everyone makes a mistake and all she needs to do is learn from it and carry on.

If she tells a lie or does not own up to her mistake - and gets caught out - her reputation as an honest person will be damaged and once that happens - it is difficult to recover that. She would probably lose her job.

If she owns up to her mistake, she would do something about it - her boss may appreciate her honesty and she would be seen as someone he could trust. Her conscience would also be clear

Benefits of zooming out when making a decision:

  • Be able to see the bigger picture
  • Consider the long term consequences
  • Do the right thing
  • Have a clean conscience
  • Consider all the options

Honesty doesn't always pay, but dishonesty always costs.

Michael Josephson

William is eating a meal with his family, looking at his phone

William is 18 and in high school. All his life is on his phone. He is constantly chatting to his friends and his phone pings all the time with notifications. He has now started bringing the phone to the dining table. When he was eating on his own he was used to looking at the phone anyway, but has now begun browsing the phone even when dining with the family. He gets angry when told to put it away, so his mother has stopped telling him to do that. He feels justified in what he is doing.

How would zooming in help William?

William is probably not aware of what he is doing and the impact of that. If he were to zoom in he would focus more on what he was doing at that moment - in this case eating his food. He could eat more slowly, be watchful of how much he is eating, enjoy his food more - and all this would be good for his health. One of the causes of obesity later in life is because we are not in tune with what we are eating, and how much.

He could also engage in a conversation with his family members and this would build their relationships and boost his communication skills.

Wherever we pay complete attention - our mind quietens and we can feel calm

Benefits of zooming in on what we are doing:

  • We can enjoy what we are doing
  • We can enjoy the beauty of nature
  • We can boost our communication skills
  • Develop a mind that is focused and calm
  • Avoid obesity

The simple act of paying attention can take you a long way.

Keanu Reeves

Peter is going through a divorce and is angry

Peter and his wife are getting divorced. They have been married for 20 years and have decided to get divorced.They have been unhappy for a long time. They have stopped communicating and are angry with each other. They are blaming each other for the state of their relationship and are now only communicating through lawyers. Peter feels he is being unfairly treated because he is being accused of not being honest about how much money he has. No one has questioned his integrity before, and this is making him really angry.

Benefits of zooming in on the feeling for Peter

When we are experiencing strong emotions such as anger it can be difficult to think and act rationally. We may feel the need to fight back in some way, seek revenge or be unkind toward others, even those who have nothing to do with the situation we are reacting to.

If Peter can zoom in and focus on the sensation of the feeling, without even naming it as anger, it can transform into a sense of calm. He can then explore the root cause, which in his case is his feeling hurt because his integrity has been questioned. Peter has always been honest in his dealings with people and he values it highly. For anyone to accuse him otherwise causes him to react angrily.

Zooming in may also allow Peter to accept his feelings as they are for now, knowing they will eventually pass. This allows him to experience his feeling of anger without reacting in the moment. Once the strong emotion has settled he can then look at the situation with fresh eyes. He could realise that his anger is a reaction from his own thinking, and that there are other ways of responding to the situation without getting angry

Benefits of zooming in on a strong emotion:

  • Accept that strong emotions are part of being human
  • Learn that the feeling comes from a reaction inside us and not from another person’s behaviour.
  • Allow us to sit with the emotion without reacting
  • You can explore different options on how to respond
  • Learning about the root cause behind the feeling brings lasting change

Simon is ambitious and works all the time

Simon is 40 and a workaholic. He works as a manager in a big company. He is driven and ambitious, starts work early, and often finishes late. He has a lot of pressure from work and is usually stressed. He has a wife and two small children. When he gets home in the evening the kids have gone to bed. On weekends he has to catch up with the work that he could not do during the week. Earning money, and what others think are important to him, as are his frequent holidays. His relationship with his wife is strained because she feels she hardly sees him. When he does have some time to himself he plays computer games to unwind. He feels he is working hard to provide for the family and their future, and she should cut him some slack.

How could zooming out help Simon

Simon’s story is a common one. Many people are conditioned to chase success, money, and recognition and are often not aware that those drivers come from their past influences.

If Simon could zoom out, which is probably difficult, he would see that time is the real currency in life. These years are not going to come back - his kids will grow up not really knowing who their dad is, and he will have a functional marriage, but not an intimate one.

He thinks there is some point in the future where he will be happy once he has achieved all his goals - but doesn’t realise that once he does get there, the pleasure from that will fade quickly - as all pleasure does.

He could re-examine what success really means - is it money, fame and expensive holidays - or does it mean being happy, less stressed, having fulfilling relationships and exploring other hobbies. He might reach the end of career, look back and wonder where all the years went, and whether it was worthwhile.

Benefits of zooming out and examining our work-life balance

  • Realise that time is the most valuable currency we have
  • We need to spend it wisely
  • Understand the hidden drivers in our thinking
  • Make conscious choices about our work-life balance
  • Explore what success really is
