The process of labelling feelings:

A feeling arises - we search our database of feeling words and assign a name to the feeling - we then look for the cause of that feeling (or what we believe is the cause) - we then look for a solution to the feeling (which may be a temporary solution to a temporary feeling) - the mind then sees the solution as a permanent fix every time a similar feeling arises - a habit is formed

Anxiety - David experiences a feeling which he labels as an anxious feeling. He wants to understand why he is experiencing anxiety. He is about to retire from work and believes that the feeling he is currently experiencing is as a result of his impending retirement. The mind searches for a solution and begins to think that he shouldn’t retire at this moment.

Relationships - Amy experiences a feeling and names it ‘frustration.’ She looks for a cause for this frustration and believes her partner david is not doing enough to help with the household chores. Amy then believes that her feeling of frustration is caused by her partner. The mind looks for the obvious solution which in this case is for Amy’s partner to change their habits so they do more to help. Amy leaves David thinking he is the cause of her unhappiness, but realises she is still unhappy.

Eating and drinking or smoking - Tony experiences a feeling which he labels stress. He believes that his stress is caused by work pressures. His mind searches for a solution to the current feeling. He is not at work at this moment but still feels stressed when he sees a bottle of wine. He thinks that a glass of wine will help him relax and get rid of the stress. Afterwards he feels more relaxed. The next time he feels a similar feeling of stress his mind will trigger a response that will have him reach for the wine to relieve the feeling. A habit begins to form where the mind’s automatic response to a feeling of stress will be to drink wine.
