#12 Exploring the masks we wear

Exercise #12

Exploring the masks we wear

Welcome. In this set of exercises we are going to explore the many masks we wear, how they form, how they impact us and how we can live in freedom without them. Keep in mind the basic principles of enquiry:

  • We are just looking and learning about ourselves
  • We need to have a mind open to learning
  • For learning to take place we need to pause judgement
  • What we observe in ourselves is not right or wrong, good or bad - it just needs to be understood
  • Deep down, we are the same human being

Why do we have masks?

Have you ever felt one thing inside, and pretended to be someone else on the outside? Or pretended to yourself that you are something you really are not? You are not alone. This is part of being human, and how our shared human mind works. This happens without our awareness.

Even if we are aware of it, we don’t seem to be able to do anything about it. Seeing this clearly can bring its own change.

The difference between who we actually are, and who we pretend to be to the world can make us feel disconnected from ourselves. We don’t feel comfortable in our own skin and are not authentic in how we present ourselves to the world.

Sometimes we need to wear these masks so we can do our job - if I am a doctor I need patients to have confidence in me - so I need to appear confident, even when I am not.

Or, we may delude ourselves and not face the truth of who we are - so never grow as human beings. I may be anxious but pretend I am not and believe that - and so my anxiety never gets addressed.

Our masks form without our awareness because we want to fit in, or do not want to accept ourselves as we are, and want to control what other people think of us.

Problems our masks can cause include:

  • Don’t feel comfortable being ourselves
  • Feel anxious about what others think
  • Feel tired keeping our masks in place
  • Do not make friends who like us for who we really are
  • Delude ourselves

Day 1

Monica pretends she is enjoying medical school

Monica is 19 and studying Medicine at University. Ever since she was young it was the dream her parents had for her - to become a doctor. She was clever and wanted to please her parents and also wanted some security in her career. She got into medical school, But was not enjoying it at all. She was drawn to the theatre and wanted to go to drama school and be an actress instead. She did not want to disappoint her parents, and was also afraid of going into the theatre with all the insecurity that life represented. So she pretended she was happy at medical school and carried on. She got progressively unhappy in her course, but did not tell her parents /

Day 1

Communicating our unhappiness is not easy

Admitting we are unhappy, even to ourselves is not easy, and communicating that to others is even harder. Our fear of the future and of change keeps us trapped in our circumstances, in this case in a career we do not enjoy. So we pretend to be happy and carry on, but inside we continue to suffer.

It needs real courage to be honest first to ourselves, and then to our loved ones, about what we really feel. In the long run it is much better to do so, rather than continue to be unhappy. This is as true of a career, a job, or a friendship.

The next time you are unhappy in a relationship, challenge yourself to accept and share how you feel, and have a conversation to deal with the issue. Do it with kindness.

Day 2

Michael is anxious, but pretends that he is fine.

Michael is 40 and works as a chartered accountant. He has suffered from anxiety for many years. He has tried many treatments with limited benefit. He puts on a mask of normality pretending to others that he is okay.

Behind the mask he continues to feel anxious and is also worried that others will find out the truth of who he really is and will think badly of him. His mask also stops him getting the help he needs to overcome it.

He hates going to work and continues to suffer behind the mask he projects to the world.

Have you ever felt anxious but pretended you were fine? How did that feel?

Day 2

Admitting we are anxious or depressed is not easy

Mental health problems are common. We often put on a brave face, pretending they do not exist - and that can make things worse. It is much better to be open about them and get the help you need.

We often hide how we really feel even from ourselves - because that challenges our image of ourselves as ‘strong’ or ‘capable’ or ‘able to sort this out’ - this is especially true of men.

Accepting how we really feel can help us explore why we feel that way, and begin a journey to freedom, and get help if we need to.

Most people are not aware they are stressed or anxious, and so continue to suffer.

The next time you feel anxious, challenge yourself to be authentic and admit that to yourself, and if appropriate, to others. This may allow you to manage it better and allow others to understand you more.

Day 3

Alex is a headteacher. He pretends to be confident, but inside feels like an impostor.

Alex is 38 and has been a school teacher for many years. He was recently appointed as a headteacher in a new school. He feels like an impostor - unsure about what to do sometimes, and feels he has to carry the expectations of many teachers and parents. He pretends to know everything about the job and works very long hours. Inside though he feels like an impostor, and gets more and more stressed. He lives with an anxiety that people will find out that he is not really up to the job

Day 3

In any situation realise you do not have to know everything. You can ask for help.

In any job we do, our fear of being judged by others creates a certain anxiety. We worry people will think less of us and we need to know everything. It is useful to realise that everyone has been in your shoes before and understands what you are going through.

Realise that asking for help is not a weakness, but a strength, because people recognise that it takes courage to do so and are more than happy to offer support.

Being authentic makes us feel vulnerable, but brings a great freedom because you can stop pretending. People usually respond to your honesty by being supportive.

Challenge yourself to be authentic and open about what you know and what you don’t. Be open to learning from others.

Day 4

Raj pretends he is successful and well connected, when he isn’t.

Raj is 42, and a self-employed businessman. He has tried to start several businesses and had limited success. When he meets other people he pretends to be more successful than he is - showing off about the people he knows or how well his business is doing. He feels compelled to do this because everyone else is doing the same. He wants people to think he is doing well and is very well connected - so he can feel important and because he thinks this will help his business. He compensates for his feeling inadequate by pretending to be more successful than he is.

Underneath his mask he is stressed and anxious and disappointed in himself and his own life.

Day 4

Pretending to be successful

We pretend to be more successful than we are, because we want to feel important and impress others. In doing that we stop being true to ourselves and lose our sense of peace. This is because our definition of success is narrow and linked to money, fame and how important we are. Success is much more than that - and includes being at peace with yourself, having excellent relationships and living with purpose.

We are competitive because being better than others makes us feel good. We cannot admit we are less than others in any way.

Being authentic allows us to be at peace with ourselves, build authentic relationships - and this can contribute to our success in the world.

Challenge yourself to be authentic - to yourself and others. Be comfortable with who you are, and your achievements - however humble they may be. This leads to a feeling of peace.

Day 5

Sophie pretends to enjoy partying with her friends

Sophie is 19 and has a group of friends she has just met at University. They enjoy partying, binge drinking and have begun experimenting with drugs like ecstasy. She pretends to enjoy this because she desperately wants to fit in, and does not want to be alone. She actually does not want to take drugs, or drink as much, but is afraid she might lose her friends if she does not go along with them.

She pretends to have a good time, but inside she feels lonely and does not feel she fits in. Her heavy drinking has now become a habit she is struggling to get rid of.

Day 5

All of us need connection and community

All of us need connection and community because we are social animals and afraid of being alone. In our desperate need to belong, we can forget who we are at times, and join a group where we cannot be ourselves and do things we are not comfortable with.

This is amplified in gang culture.

Being authentic allows us to attract the right kind of people to join us. In this environment we can flourish and grow as human beings.

It does need the courage to stand alone if necessary though. It may feel difficult, but it is much better than feeling you don’t fit in all the time.

Challenge yourself to be authentic, be yourself, and speak up about what you like and don’t like in a group. You will attract people who like you for who you really are.

Day 6

Max is a psychologist. He hides his sadness and drinking problem - even from himself.

Max is 18 and at university, studying psychology. He’s had an unhappy childhood. His parents split up about 5 years ago, and he does not get on with his new stepdad. To deal with his sadness he started drinking when he was 15, and naturally became friends with others who drank regularly.

Now at university his drinking has become a problem - he misses lectures, does not shower regularly and wants to part as much as he can. He has failed a term exam but not told his parents. He refuses to acknowledge his sadness and instead tries to cover it up by drinking. He knows he has a drinking problem but is unable to stop. To his friends he is the life of the party, and he often boasts that he can out drink his friends, but his deep sadness continues.

Day 6

Acknowledging the truth of who we are is not easy, but brings freedom

Acknowledging the truth of who we really are is not easy, but the first step in dealing with a problem we may have and growing as a human being.

We think that accepting the truth of who we are is going to be more painful, or we do not think there is a way out. Alcohol or drugs can help us numb the pain - but the problem is never dealt with so never goes away. Soon the habit takes over and we are trapped - and continue even if the original sadness recedes.

Dealing with the situation head on, often by getting help, however painful it may be, is the best way of moving forwards - and avoids us falling into the trap of addiction

Day 7

Sam pretends to be strong, but actually feels insecure

Sam is 16 and has had a tough life. His father left when he was very young and he was brought up by his mother who had problems with alcohol. He was bullied when he was younger.

He feels he needs to show he is strong, but inside he feels insecure. He wants to control the behavior of others to make himself feel more secure, and often does that through bullying. Bullying others also feels like a release of his own inner tension, and he enjoys that sense of power it gives him. He thinks by controlling the behavior of others, he is less likely to get hurt, but does not realise that he is hurting other people at the same time.

Day 7

Dealing with our insecurity is not easy, but brings freedom

Feeling insecure is part of being human. We try to deal with it by controlling the people in our life because we are afraid we will get hurt. In doing so however we can damage our relationships and behave in ways that hurt others.

Rather than deal with our own insecurities, we sometimes compensate by blaming others for how we feel, and try to control their behaviour.

Wisdom is realising that others are not responsible for our insecurity - and it comes from ourselves. We cannot keep someone’s heart a prisoner. A complete acceptance of the fact that people in our lives are not in a prison, and are free to leave brings freedom - though this is not easy to do. It also brings freedom for the other person - and a conversation about this can bring you closer together.

Challenge yourself to take ownership of your own insecurities, and use your wisdom to understand why, instead of blaming others. You will grow as a person if you do.

Uncertainty is the only certainty there is, and learning to live with it is the only security.

John Paulos
