Introducing

The importance of questioning what we think

Welcome.

The quality of our lives depends on the quality of our thinking. This exercise, and in fact the entire Human Wisdom program aims to improve the quality of your thinking

We are going to explore the importance of being able to doubt our assumptions and question what we think and feel. This is not something that comes naturally to us. We find this difficult because we feel so certain about what we think and feel. Our mind is like that.

This ability to question our own thinking can be life changing.

The important thing is not to stop questioning.

Einstein

Our mind assumes what it thinks and feels is true. Being able to question our assumptions is the hallmark of a wise person.

Day 1

Simon and Laura have parents who argue a lot

Simon is 14 and his sister Laura is 12. They live with their parents. Their Dad works as a baker and their mom is a homemaker. Dad works long hours and when he gets home he is tired and often likes to flop in front of the TV. Mom ends up doing all the house work and cooks the evening meal for them all.

Recently they have noticed their parents arguing a lot. When their Dad gets home the house is often a mess and he complains about it. Their Mom says there is too much to do to keep on top of everything and they end up having an argument. She expects him to contribute to some house work and he says he is too tired to do that.

These arguments have gotten worse recently and made them feel unsettled. They have become anxious that their parents may split up and the atmosphere is the house is strained.

Day 1

How could Simon and Laura respond?

This is a common situation in many homes. How can Simon and Laura respond? They could realize that arguments between parents are not uncommon, and it is part of many adult relationships. It is understandable to be upset when parents argue, but they could try and not let it affect them.

In this situation, Simon and Laura could begin to question their assumptions. The biggest one being that the other person is responsible for my unhappiness.

They could also try and help their Mom around the house. It is always a transition from being a child where everything is done for you, to someone who does things for others they care about. That would ease the pressure on their Mom and perhaps reduce the arguments their parents have.

Our mind naturally assumes the worst possible scenario,feels certain of this, and gets anxious. Realizing that we do not have to believe every thought to be true, allows us space to step back, examine it, and explore other ways of looking at the same situation.

It is normal for you to feel worried when parents argue. Realizing that most of our worries never come to pass can help us to be less anxious.

Day 2

Sarah is stressed by school.

Sarah is 14 and in high school. She finds school stressful. Her friends Neha and Amelia are at the same school. Sarah finds the homework too much, the tests stressful, and she is always worrying about what other people are thinking about her. She isn’t as clever as Neha, though she gets better grades than Amelia. Her stress is making her unhappy, and not feel like coming to school. No matter how much her parents try and talk to her about why she is stressed, her stress continues.

She notices that her friends Neha and Amelia don’t appear to be as stressed and are happier, but she cannot understand why

Day 2

How could Sarah be less stressed?

How could Sarah change the way she thinks, and could that help her be less stressed? Stress is caused by the automatic way our mind reacts to its circumstances - in this case her school.

She could ask why her friends who go to the same school are not stressed. She assumes her stress comes from school, and what others think of her, but perhaps her stress comes from the way her mind is reacting to the school, but she cannot see that.

Perhaps she could just accept that the school is the same for everyone, and by having a positive attitude she could reduce her stress. For example, she could be grateful to have an education in the first place - so many young people in the world don’t have that luxury. She could ask her friends how they manage to not be stressed, and learn from them. And she could explore what's going on in her thinking to make her react in this way, and if there is another way of responding to the situation. She could work harder to get better grades if she wants to, or accept the grades she gets

By accepting things as they are, and being grateful for what she has, her stress could reduce significantly. All this needs is a mind that is open to looking at things from a different point of view

Stress is just the difference between how things are and how we want them to be. By questioning our thoughts on how we want things to be, our stress can be reduced.

The greatest weapon against stress is our ability to choose one thought over another.

William James.

Day 3

David’s girlfriend she wants to leave him

David is 18 and just finishing high school and all set to go to university in 2 months. He has been dating his girlfriend Amy for 3 years. They have got into different universities - he is going to Edinburgh, and she is going to London. Over a coffee Amy tells him that she no longer loves him and wants to end their relationship. She wants a fresh start when she goes to university.

He is devastated and in shock. He really loved Amy and he can’t seem to accept it. His initial shock gives way to denial, anger, and a lack of self-confidence. He feels he will not find love again. Why would people find him attractive anyway? He is angry at Amy, critical of himself for not making the relationship work, and starts feeling depressed.

Day 3

Why is there so much pain when relationships break up? How can we think about this differently?

When relationships end so much pain and conflict usually follows. Why can the ending of relationships not be peaceful? What is behind all the anger and sadness that follows? We rely on relationships to meet our long list of emotional needs - to be loved, understood, for security, to feel valued and as a balm for our loneliness. When they end we are suddenly faced with our own emptiness, fears for the future, and a feeling we are not valued - this hurt quickly turns to anger, and we blame others and want to punish them for making us feel this way.

Here is another way of seeing the same situation. A relationship is not a prison. People do have the freedom to leave. Would you like that freedom for yourself if you were unhappy? Yes - it is natural to be sad - but that sadness comes from within me - my fear, loneliness and feelings of not being loved. I will deal with that and recover. If I have loved this person who wants to go, I’m going to let them go with my blessings so they can find happiness where they go. There is no point being with someone who does not want to be with me. It's an opportunity to take stock and look to the future. I will not wallow in self-pity or allow anger or bitterness to get the better of me. I want to see what I can learn from this relationship and try to be a better person - and maybe another relationship will follow. I will see.

If we are open and willing to question our assumptions, each relationship teaches us something new about ourselves and allows us to grow as a person - especially the relationships that do not work out.

Day 4

Amy has social anxiety.

We can make assumptions about what others are thinking and this can lead to anxiety. Consider Amy’s story.

Amy is 20. She finished high school but has not gone on to college or to get a job. She has been at home for the last 18 months. She is really anxious about what other people think about her and it's her social anxiety that has kept her at home. When she is around other people she gets so stressed that she feels she cannot breathe properly - and so feels it’s better to stay at home. The thought of meeting new people or being in a group fills her with dread. Her parents have tried to get her to get some help, but she has refused. Her relationship with her parents gets strained

Day 4

How can Amy feel less anxious?

How can Amy feel less anxious?

As a first step Amy could realize that her anxiety is linked to her thinking because when she is thinking about something else her anxiety eases.

She could then begin to question her assumptions. Are people really thinking about her as she thinks? Or, are they too busy thinking about themselves?

And why does she assume they are thinking bad things about her? That may not be true. Why does it matter so much anyway? The people she is close to love her anyway - and you cannot be liked by everyone - after all - she doesn’t like everyone she meets - so why should others?

She could also ask if she likes herself - and if not - what gets in the way? Or - is she waiting for others to like her before she likes herself?

All these questions will help Amy to let go of her social anxiety.

Day 5

Steven is angry

Our mind assumes our anger comes from the outside. Consider Steven’s story.

Steven is 55 and works as an executive in a bank. He has a short temper and he knows it. He tries to control it but often that does not work. He gets angry when driving to work if he thinks people are driving poorly, or in a restaurant if he thinks the service is too slow.

He gets angry with his wife when he goes home if the house is not clean and tidy. If the children are making too much noise or watching too much television he tells them off. He also gets angry if they do not get the grades he expects of them in their exams. He thinks he is doing this for their own good. He is angry at work if he feels his junior staff are not working hard enough. Everyone seems scared of him - even his children. He realizes his anger makes him stressed, but he just wishes people would step up and do what they needed to do.

Day 5

How can Steven be less angry?

How can Steven become less angry by questioning his assumptions?

The first assumption Steven needs to question is that his anger comes from the outside - and the way the world is. It is natural for the mind to assume this - but it is not true. His anger arises when there is a difference between how things are, and how he wants them to be. He does not see this. This could make him take responsibility for his anger and at least not blame others for it. If he did want to change something or express his displeasure he can do it in a kind way and without getting angry.

Realizing that his anger comes from his own expectations on how things should be could help him to question these assumptions and let many of them go - and accept things as they are. Everyone is different and doing their best. If he was kinder, he may get the best out of people

Our mind assumes our anger comes from the outside and we are quick to blame others for it. But in truth it comes from our own unmet expectations and our own disappointment. If the world is not as we want it, we can get angry. The patterns of anger often repeat themselves.

Think of a common cause of your own anger. Is there a different way of thinking about the same situation?

Where there is anger, there is always pain underneath

Eckhart Tolle

Day 6

Josh thinks he is not clever.

Josh is 17 and in high school. He finds school life tough. He has lost interest in school because he does not think he is clever and his friends Sarah and James get better grades than him. He hates exams and the inevitable comparing of grades when the results come out. ‘What did you get?’ - is one one of his most dreaded questions. The Maths teacher has told him on more than one occasion that he needs to try harder - but he feels ‘Why bother’ - because he will never be as good as his friends. He studies enough to get through the exams but finds them stressful. Because he is not interested in studying he plays up in the class, and tries to be funny because he is just bored. This gets him into trouble - and he dislikes school even more. He can’t wait to get out of school.

Day 6

How can Josh look at things differently?

How can Josh feel better about himself and his time in school?

He could begin by questioning his assumption that he is not clever. He could realize that just because others have better grades than him does not mean he is not clever. Perhaps he could work harder, or ask more questions in class if he does not understand something.

He could realize that his mind is comparing itself with others all the time and feeling bad if others are better. This does not serve him - and is making him dislike himself, dislike others, and lose interest in the subject. He could also accept that exam grades are not a great predictor of how well people do in life - character is perhaps more important. As long as he does his best he could be happy with whatever grades he gets. He could realize that being disruptive in class is not being kind to others who want to learn, and he could change. What do you think?

We assume that we are not good enough because others are better, and convince ourselves we will never be able to do something we actually would like to do or achieve. This is worth questioning.

Don’t stop yourself from greatness because of fear or self-doubt. You were put here on this planet to do great things.

Kaiden Blake

Believe you can, and you are half-way there

Theodore Roosevelt

#5 Tips on how to question your assumptions.

Tip 1

Realize that having assumptions is not wrong. They feel real, but may not be true.

5 Tips on how to question your assumptions.

Tip 2

They are an automatic response from your many past influences, which we are not aware of.

5 Tips on how to question your assumptions.

Tip 3

Be curious. Have an open mind. Explore the assumptions you make about yourself and others.

5 Tips on how to question your assumptions.

Tip 4

Ask yourself a simple question - Is there another way of thinking about this situation that may also be true? Write that down.

5 Tips on how to question your assumptions.

Tip 5

Be kind to yourself. Is there a kinder, more generous way of thinking about yourself or others? You are just human and it is okay to feel what you do.

We don’t question our own assumptions. When others question us we feel criticized and get defensive, so our patterns of thinking and behavior continue.

Benefits of questioning your thoughts and feelings:

  • Break the boundaries of what you think you can do
  • Feel calmer - not assuming what others are thinking
  • Manage your emotions - by questioning the thinking behind them
  • Have happier relationships - by not assuming others are responsible for your happiness
  • Be less stressed - by questioning how you want things to be
  • Be less anxious - by questioning whether the danger is real
