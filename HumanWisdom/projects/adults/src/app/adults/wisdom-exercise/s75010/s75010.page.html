Exercise #9

Looking without thinking

Welcome to this session where we are going to learn an amazing skill - how to be with a thought or feeling without naming it or reacting to it. This has many benefits which we will explore. Keep in mind the basic principles of enquiry:

  • Be curious - we are just looking and learning about ourselves
  • For learning to take place we need to pause judgement
  • What we observe in ourselves is not right or wrong, good or bad
  • Deep down, we are all the same human beings

Introduction - Learn an amazing new skill

This may seem like a very strange idea at first - but learning to notice a thought or feeling without reacting to it is a skill that everyone can learn and it can quite simply revolutionise your life, creating space for wisdom to operate.

We know our mind is buzzing with thoughts - around 6000 per day. Each thought triggers other thoughts, and can lead to spontaneous action - often without our awareness. We may begin with a thought that we are not good enough, and it may trigger a series of other thoughts which make us feel bad about ourselves.

We have also explored how our feelings are powerful, arise spontaneously, and demand action - we react to anger or desire or loneliness in ways that may not serve us in the long term.

In this session we are going to explore how we can look at a thought or feeling as it rises, just watching it and not reacting to it in any way. Every thought or feeling fades away - and this can give us space to consider if we need to act, and what is the right thing to do.

To look at a thought or feeling and do nothing about it needs a mind that is sensitive, quiet, and alert. This simple practice opens the door to wisdom and right action

Can you observe a thought or feeling, as you would watch a leaf fall from a tree, or a bird in flight - just noticing it, without thinking about it.

Of all the skills you will learn, this can feel the most challenging. It's simple, but not easy. Open your heart to learn it and do it with passion. It can transform your life.

Benefits of looking without acting/reacting

There are many benefits of learning to look at a feeling or thought without thinking, without labelling, or reacting, or judging, or blaming someone. If you are feeling angry, being able to observe that anger rising in you and doing nothing about it gives you options to consider the best response. Every feeling has a beginning and an ending. Observing your feelings without acting on them also allows you to learn about their origin. For example your anger comes from a feeling of being hurt, or disappointed.

Similarly observing your thinking allows you to communicate with care. There is a tremendous power in pausing before reacting to any situation.

It can help you break a habit - watching the urge to reach for your phone, and just continuing to watch it and not give in to it weakens the habit eventually.

It may stop you buying things you do not need - you could watch that thought which says - 'I want this’ and ask yourself if you really need it.

It can also help you deal with strong emotions like grief. You can then deal with it without escaping into the clutches of alcohol or drugs.

Some benefits of looking at a thought/feeling and not reacting:

  • Manage your emotions
  • Break an unhelpful habit
  • Deal with stress and grief
  • Avoid addiction when emotionally distressed
  • Speak with more care
  • Stop buying what you do not need

A simple breathing exercise to quieten your mind

Practice this simple breathing exercise several times a day, whenever you have a few minutes to yourself. Each time you do it your mind will quieten, making it easier to notice your thoughts and feelings as they arise. It is also helpful while watching a thought or feeling because it makes the mind go quiet.

Begin by placing your right hand on your chest. Take a deep breath through your nose, filling your lungs, and let it out. Now breathe in slowly to a count of 4 1-2-3-4, and breathe out slowly to a count of 6 1-2-3-4-5-6. Notice how your chest rises and falls. The breathing out needs to be slow and steady. As you breathe out, notice the air coming out of your nostrils is warmer than the air going in.

Do this for as long as you can manage - 10 minutes is a good time to sit quietly and do this - but you can also do it for a few minutes between your daily activities - in the bus, or while driving to work, or before you sleep at night.

Each time you do this you feel calm and your mind quietens.

What does looking without action involve?

  • Noticing a thought or feeling
  • Not naming it
  • Not thinking about it
  • Not resisting it
  • Not giving in to it
  • Not judging it as right or wrong
  • Connecting with your breathing
  • Noticing how your mind quietens

The moment you label a thought or feeling, or resist it, or give in to it, you lose touch with it. You stop seeing the flower in front of you once you label it as a rose, or you stop seeing the tree once you call it an oak tree.

Overcoming smartphone addiction

Smartphone addiction is the commonest form of addiction on earth - though most people would not call it that.

A UK research study reported that 39% of 18-30 year olds had a smartphone addiction and this impacted their ability to sleep, socialise with friends, reduced productivity, contributed to obesity, and accidents while driving.

Are you unable to control your smartphone use, keep it on all the time, and get anxious when it's not nearby? You are not alone.

Learning to observe the urge to pick up the phone and just observing it without giving it to it or suppressing it is not easy - but that can help you break that habit

As you notice the urge to pick up the phone, and stay with that feeling, becoming conscious of your breathing, your mind quietens. You create space to decide if you really need to pick up the phone.

Digital addiction is going to be one of the great mental health crises of our time

Sam Altman

The key is to notice, and not resist the feeling, but not to give into it either. Being able to notice the feeling or thought that makes you reach for the phone, and stay with it, brings its own change.

Exploring your eating and drinking habits

The urge to eat or have a fizzy drink or some alcohol is powerful and one we find difficult to control. Overeating or drinking too much can become a habit - and this is driven by powerful emotions in us that we are not aware of. Presented with food or drink we are often unable to control how much we consume.

As a result there is an obesity epidemic globally - affecting 42% of Americans and 28% of people in the UK - this can lead to diabetes, heart disease and other problems. The World Health Organization says excessive alcohol consumption causes 3 million deaths/year and is linked to over 200 diseases.

What is going on in our thinking to make us unable to manage our habits, despite our best efforts, and what can we do about it?

Noticing habits around food

Just as we have brought our attention to bear on our urge to speak we are going to do the same to food and drink.

Do you eat with awareness - or are you thinking of something else as you eat. Notice how when you start drinking alcohol, your control of how much you are drinking starts to slip.

Notice also the feelings which drive your habits - your unconscious need to be occupied, or your need for pleasure - or as a way of dealing with stress or anxiety.

Regard all these strong unconscious urges as opportunities for learning a new skill. The key is to notice your unconscious habits around food and drink - without any resistance. The goal is not to control, but just to notice, and then stay with that feeling without naming it

Noticing our unconscious habits around food and drink is simple, but not easy, and requires perseverance. As you notice the beginning of the habit, tune into your breathing and notice how the urge fades away, and your mind quietens.

Your environment can trigger your habits without you being aware of it. For example, if the biscuits are on the table in front of you, you are more likely to eat them.

By becoming aware of our habits around food and drink we can eat and drink in moderation and avoid the problems of not doing so.

The root cause of obesity and excessive alcohol consumption is in our thinking - in our unconscious habits, in our need for stimulation and pleasure, and as a balm for our feelings of stress and anxiety. The answer lies in a deeper awareness of what is going on.

Exploring anxiety

Anxiety affects between 20 and 40% of people. We know it is caused by our thinking because when we distract ourselves, the anxiety abates for a while.

No matter how hard we try not to be anxious, the anxiety persists. We don’t seem to be able to control it. Our mind assumes our anxiety comes from external events. A mind that is prone to anxiety finds no shortage of things to worry about and is certain its worries are genuine.

The feeling of anxiety is very real, but the danger it points to may not be - and often isn’t. The mind is wired to protect us from danger, but the danger is often not real.

In this exercise we are going to approach the feeling of anxiety in a different way - by first noticing it in ourselves. Then just watching it without thinking it’s right or wrong, or trying to escape from it or find a solution.

We are going to let it rise, tune into our breathing, and not do anything about it - you could also say we are going to try and meet it without thinking.

If we can meet the feeling of anxiety without thinking, we can cut off its oxygen supply and it can dissolve into a sense of silence - which gives us options to consider.

This is not easy to do - but definitely possible - let’s give it a try.

Anxiety has its roots in thinking. If we can meet it completely, without thinking, it can dissolve.

When we pay complete attention to a feeling, it transforms into silence. This gives our mind space to understand what is behind the feeling, and respond with wisdom.

Remember - the feeling of anxiety is real, but the danger it is pointing to may not be. When this is seen clearly, it can dissolve.

Stay with your feeling of emptiness or loneliness

To be human is to feel the ache of loneliness sometimes. This can come on even when you are with people. Sometimes we call it a feeling of emptiness. Most of modern life is set up to avoid this feeling if we can. We have endless digital entertainment. Despite this, the ache of loneliness or emptiness does not go away and follows us like a shadow we try to escape from all the time. This leaves us with no sense of peace.

In this exercise we are going to approach this differently. We are going to stay with this feeling when it rises, tune into our breathing gently, and neither suppress it or escape from it. This is not easy, but there are many many benefits of doing so.

Firstly, you will always be comfortable in your own company - and be able to form relationships based on a genuine shared interest, rather than just as a band aid for your loneliness. It will stop you from doing things just to escape from this feeling - shopping for things you don’t need, taking drugs or alcohol for example. Most importantly though you will live with a feeling of peace within.

Let's get going.

Staying with your feeling of loneliness can help you:

  • Always be comfortable being with yourself
  • Avoid escaping through shopping, alcohol, or drugs
  • Live with a sense of peace
  • Form meaningful connections with others

Escaping from our loneliness is neither right or wrong, good or bad. It's just something to explore. On the other side of that feeling lies peace.

Notice this feeling of loneliness/emptiness when it rises in you. Stay with it. Do not resist it, or give in to it, or judge it, or even name it. Just be with it, tuning into your breathing as you do so. Share what happened.

Before you put on the TV, or browse the internet, or go shopping, or reach for a drink - PAUSE - and notice the feeling behind that urge. Don’t name it, or resist it, or give in to it - tune into your breathing and just stay with that feeling. What happened?

If you can stay with the ache of loneliness or emptiness, and meet that feeling completely without naming it, or escaping from it, or judging it - it dissolves into a sense of peace.

Of all the skills you can learn, to meet a feeling or thought without language or resistance can feel the most challenging. Stick with it anyway. It can transform your life.

Meeting emotional pain

We use the term emotional pain here to cover the feeling of hurt we feel when we are criticized, or the grief we feel when we lose what we have loved, or if a memory of pain from the past surfaces and clouds our present.

This pain can feel intense and it feels like a fire in our brain. Our natural instinct is to escape from any fire. Sometimes our escapes make things worse - if we turn to alcohol, or drugs or gambling. Opioid addiction killed 100,000 people in the US in 2021 and for many people this underlying reason was an emotional pain they were trying to escape from.

It is challenging, but if we can stay with that feeling of pain as it rises in our mind and body, and not escape from it, meeting it without thinking, the fire can dissolve into an inner silence. This then creates the space to heal.

We can then explore the underlying causes for that in our own thinking. For example, behind our feeling of grief on the death of a loved one could be our loneliness, our fear for the future, our guilt or a change in our material circumstances - and deal with each of these.

Explore the module on Looking without Language in the Art of Enquiry section of the app.

Benefits of meeting our emotional pain without resistance or language:

  • We do not escape to alcohol, or drugs to numb the pain
  • We can deal with the underlying causes of it
  • Our heart can begin to heal

Staying with the pain as it arises can help us heal more quickly and move on with our lives.

People go through so much pain trying to avoid pain.

Neil Strauss
