Exercise #11

Who am I?

Welcome. In this set of exercises we are going to explore our various identities, how they form, and how these impact our lives and shape the world. Keep in mind the basic principles of enquiry:

  • We are just looking and learning about ourselves
  • We need to have a mind open to learning
  • For learning to take place we need to pause judgement
  • What we observe in ourselves is not right or wrong, good or bad - it just needs to be understood
  • Deep down, we are the same human being

Exploring your identity. Why bother?

Whenever you say ‘I am’… you are identifying yourself with something outside yourself - whether it's a country, a religion, or a political group.

Welcome to this set of exercises where we are going to explore our many identities, how they form, how they impact our lives in ways we are not aware of, and the many benefits of exploring the subject.

So why bother? Our identities determine who we think we are, what others think of us, who we form relationships with and whom we have conflicts with. They may determine where we feel comfortable living, and who we feel comfortable being with. They influence what we think of others, and how we feel - for example if our team loses an international tournament.

As humans our different identities have caused wars since the beginning of history.

Understanding our identity may allow us to live in peace with ourselves and others

Benefits of exploring our identity

  • Be comfortable with who we are
  • Understand how others see us
  • Connect with others who are different
  • Make better decisions
  • Avoid conflict

Nothing of me is original. I am the combined effort of everyone I have ever known.

Chuck Palahniuk

Most people are other people. Their thoughts are someone else’s opinions. Their lives a mimicry.

Oscar Wilde

How do identities form? Meet Avi and Rehana

How do our identities form?

To explore this and other questions around identity we are going to travel with Avi and Rehana, both studying English literature in New York university.

Rehana’s parents are Palestinian and from the West Bank - she came to the US when she was 16. She is muslim, believes in the rights of women to choose their future, and politically, identifies herself as a democrat. Even though she lives in the US, she identifies herself as Palestinian

Avi is Jewish, and in Rehana’s class. His parents are from Israel, and emigrated when he was 10. He identifies himself as an American, a republican, and believes abortion should not be allowed. He is a keen basketball player and in the college team. A lot of his life revolves around basketball. He supports the New York Knicks.

How do we acquire our identities?

We acquire our identities without our awareness from our various conditioning influences. If we are born in India we will say we are Indian, for example. If we have parents who are Muslim, we are most likely to call ourselves Muslim, and so on.

We also identify ourselves according to our gender, our sexual orinentation, our political views, our skin colour, or the sports team we support.

As we will explore, these identities impact us in several ways

Each of us can have several identities based on:

  • Nationality
  • Religion
  • Political views
  • Skin colour
  • Gender
  • Sexual orientation
  • Our profession
  • Being a parent

Our identities form without our awareness, or our permission. It is just the way our mind works. Be aware of all your identities without justifying them, or thinking they are wrong. Observe how they operate in your life.

What are the different ways our identity can shape our lives?

  • What we eat, and how we live
  • Who we like and don’t like
  • Where we pray
  • Who we have relationships with
  • Create our prejudices
  • Who we go to war with

Avi and Rehana are attached to their identities

Both Avi and Rehana are attached to their own identities in different ways

One day in class someone was being critical of Muslims, saying the recent terrorist bombings were perpetrated by people in the name of Jihad. She found herself feeling really defensive, as if she was being attacked herself - and found herself saying that may not be true, and it may just be a plot by others to make muslims look bad.

Avi on the other hand found himself feeling defensive when there was a news item about how Israel had bombed the Gaza strip and innocent women and children had died. He found himself saying that Israel had a right to self-defence.

Do you think Avi and Rehana are aware of their attachment to their identities?

Are you attached to your identities? Do you feel defensive when an identity you have is criticised? Are there other ways this attachment manifests?

How does our attachment to our identities manifest?

It is common for most of us to become attached to our respective identities. This happens without our awareness and can manifest in different ways.

If someone is critical about the country or religion we identify with we can feel as if we are being personally criticised, and can get defensive.

We struggle to accept that the country or religion or political leader we identify with has done anything wrong - because that would mean we have done something wrong ourselves.

If someone is attacking a country who shares my religion, I can feel angry with that country because I feel personally attacked.

Equally, if someone praises my religion or country, I can feel a glow of pride in myself, or if a team from my country is playing a football tournament, I can feel I am playing that game myself - and feel overjoyed if my team wins- as if I had won the match myself.

Our attachment to our identities can manifest in different ways:

  • We get defensive when our country or religion is criticised
  • We feel attacked personally when our religion or country is threatened
  • We feel a burst of pleasure when our country ‘wins’, or someone praises it
  • We want to support people from our religion or our country, over others

Why do you think we get attached to our identities? We may live in a certain street, but that is unlikely to become part of our identity. Why?

Why do we get attached to our identities?

This attachment to our various identities happens without our awareness. We are not aware it is happening or why - we just feel attached - and never question that attachment either, since everyone around us is behaving in the same way.

We get attached to anything that meets some emotional need in us - whether it is a person, or a pet - or an identity.

So - what are the emotional needs we have that having an identity can meet? We perhaps feel more secure being part of a group - which we think will look after us, and we don’t feel alone. Or a religion may make us feel less afraid of the future, or help us when we feel down.

Or supporting a basketball team may bring us a burst of pleasure every time they win.

Avi and Rehana want to be with people they identify with

As Avi and Rehana get settled into college and begin looking for friends, they find themselves naturally drawn to people they identify with.

Rehana joins the Muslim Students Association, and Avi joins the Jewish Students association. Most of her friends are other students from the middle-east. Most of Avi’s friends are white and Jewish.

In their groups they feel comfortable and supported and don’t feel alone. They feel people will understand them. People agree with their opinions and never challenge them, so they get reinforced. Their prejudices against people who are different get reinforced as well. Most of these prejudices are unconscious.

The problem of being with people like us

All over the world people who share an identity flock together in small tribes. We may have one tribe for each of our identities. If I am a dentist, Muslim and British, and living in Spain, I may join the local British community, but also join the local Muslim association and the local dental association.

We do feel more comfortable surrounded by people like us. There is nothing wrong with that, but it can contribute to conflict in the world. Each tribe can become suspicious of another tribe, wants to gather power for itself, and there is the possibility of misunderstanding and conflict.

If you look at all the conflicts in the world, whether it's gang warfare in cities, or conflict between religious groups, or countries, they are mostly between people of one identity vs another.

The natural human tendency of wanting to be with people who share our identity contributes to conflict in the world

Problems being surrounded by people like us can cause:

  • Our opinions and prejudices can get reinforced
  • We become suspicious of people who are different
  • Conflict can occur between different groups
  • We find it difficult to cooperate with those who are different
  • We cannot see things from another person’s point of view

Continue noticing how you feel when you meet people who are similar to you, and when you meet those who are different. Do this without judgement.

If you want to plant a seed of peace in the world, build bridges with people with different identities.

Avi and Rehana meet for the first time

Avi and Rehana notice each other in the English class but hardly speak. Rehana is tall and beautiful and Avi finds her attractive. Rehana notices that Avi is a basketball star and all the other girls seem to want to be with him. She notices he is kind and finds that quality attractive.

They are assigned to the same project group so meet and begin speaking to each other.

They both make assumptions about each other based on their identities. Avi assumes that because Rehana talks about the rights of Palestinians she supports terrorism, and Rehana assumes that because Avi talks about Israel’s right to defend itself, he supports the killing of civilians.

Do you make assumptions about others based on their identities? How do you know you are right?

What assumptions do you think others make about you, based on your identity?

The assumptions we make about others are automatic and unconscious

We automatically make assumptions about other people based on their identities and others do the same to us.

If I am an Indian and see people from Pakistan I make assumptions about them based on everything I have read and heard over my lifetime. Because the two countries have been in conflict for so long, there feels like a natural division between us.

The assumptions we make are often not accurate, and stop us getting close to others.

These assumptions were responsible for the 2 million people who died when India and Pakistan were created as separate countries in 1947.

As a result of our various identities, we stop seeing the person in front of us clearly.//

Notice how your mind automatically makes assumptions about people you meet based on their identities. Can you put those assumptions to one side, and get to know the actual person you are meeting?

Avi and Rehana meet for coffee, and get into an argument.

Avi musters up the courage to ask Rehana out for a cup of coffee one day. Reluctantly, she agrees.

Initially they are polite, but as they meet more often their arguments increase. Rehana cannot get past Avi’s opposition to abortion, and his support for the republican party or Israel, and Avi tries to convince her of his point of view which he thinks is correct. He cannot understand her support of the Democratic party, and her pro-abortion stance for example.

They try to change each other’s opinions and that just increases their conflict.

Though they find each other attractive, their many differences make them judge each other based on their attachment to their respective identities. They cannot see how they could ever be together, or even close friends.

When we meet people who are different, we try to change them to be more like us, because that makes us feel more comfortable. This creates more resistance and conflict.

All our identities are an unconscious creation of our conditioning and our thinking. They can divide us from others and are behind much of the conflict in the world. Seeing this clearly allows us to look beyond our differences, and connect as human beings.

Avi is thrown out of the basketball team

Being part of the basketball team is a strong part of Avi’s identity - one he thrives on.

Recently his performance has not been great and younger team members have been better. He was shocked when his coach told him that he was losing his place in the team.

He felt an intense emotional pain, like something really important had been stripped away from him. He feels depressed, a bit lost, and suddenly he is no longer popular with the girls.

He is angry with the coach, and himself, and doesn’t know what to do

Our identities meet our emotional needs, and when we lose them, we experience emotional pain. The more we are attached to our identities, the more the chances of feeling this pain.

Avi and Rehana have a moment of clarity and insight

Rehana sees Avi looking sad and invites him for a coffee. He tells her about the place he lost in the team.

They feel drawn to each other and continue meeting.

They gradually realise that they have much more in common than they first thought. They both like Italian food, being in nature, keeping fit, and the poems of Mary Oliver.

In the middle of an argument one day Rehana has a real insight - a spark of light in a dark room. She suddenly realises that all their arguments are about their differences - and these differences have come from their unconscious conditioning influences, such as the family or country they were born in - which has formed their identity to which they are attached.

She shares this with Avi - who has a similar moment of clarity.

Gradually they start changing, and falling in love.

Avi and Rehana tell their parents

Their relationship continues to blossom. They no longer try to change each others, instead they make an effort to see things from each others point of view.

One day they go on their favourite hike and at the top Avi gets down on one knee and proposes. Rehana has no hesitation in saying yes.

They decide to tell their parents - and it does not go down well. All the prejudices which were present when they first met are amplified in their parents. Avi’s parents recall the many terrorist attacks in Israeli cities. Rehana’s parents cannot forget the attacks on civilians by the israeli army. And there there is the issue of their different religions.

Avi and Rehana marry

Despite all the objections from their parents, Avi and Rehana get married. They are surrounded by their friends and brothers and sisters but their parents refuse to come to the wedding, or speak to each other. It's a sad occasion, but Avi and Rehana feel so happy together they cannot imagine being with anyone else.

They go on to have two children, and gradually there is a melting of hearts hardened by years of conditioning. The parents eventually accept their marriage, mainly because they want to be part of the grandchildren's lives.

You are far more than your identities. Beyond them all lies peace.

We assume we are our identities - I am British, Christian, White, Working-Class, A Liverpool Football Supporter, A Dentist, A Mother, President of the local Rotary club, and so on….

We are far more than all these identities - they cover the extraordinary human being that we are underneath all of them.

Not only do our identities divide us from others, and sow conflict in the world, but they also stop us from connecting with our true selves, where we feel at peace.

To discover this peace for ourselves and connect deeply with others, we need to understand how our identities form, understand our emotional needs, and consciously let our attachment to our identities go - one by one……

When we are asked to let go of our identity, or our attachment to it, we can experience a reaction - of fear, or anger or a feeling of being attacked.

What does it mean to lose our attachment to our identity?

We assume that losing our attachment to our identity will lead to suffering. Who will I be if I am not a Hindu, or Nigerian, or a Republican, for example?

Rehana gradually loses her attachment to her various identities and so does Avi. Rehana still goes to the mosque and Avi to the synagogue, and they still have their different political views, but because they have lost their attachment to their identities and points of view, they don’t feel they have to defend them and get others to think like them, and can connect as the human beings that they are. They still love their customs, and food, and traditions - it’s just that they do not divide them any more.

Losing their attachment to their identities does not mean they have lost their identities - just that they no longer need to defend them and they do not divide them from others.

Letting go of our attachment to an identity is easier if we can get our emotional needs met elsewhere, perhaps through our relationships, and understand how these formed in the first place.

How do you think the world would change if we could all let go of our attachment to our respective identities?

Freedom from identity allows you to connect with the extraordinary human being that you are, and brings peace.

Letting go of our attachment to all our identities brings tremendous freedom. We no longer feel divided from others who are different.

We no longer feel triggered when someone criticises our country of birth or the religion we practise.

We can connect with others who are different with an open curiosity and those relationships can enrich our lives.

We can be at peace with ourselves, knowing that underneath that we are the same human being as everyone else and that brings a deeper sense of peace

Carry this awareness of identity and how it operates in your life as you travel through your life. Ask questions. Keep an open mind. Discover the beauty and freedom of being free for yourself.
